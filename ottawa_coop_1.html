Canada Border Services Agency

For my first Co-op I completed an 8-month work term at Canada Border Service Agency in their HQ offices. Working for the border is a very important job. Millions of people come into Canada everyday using our land points of entry, by sea, or by air landing at an Airport. Canada's Border officers make use of technology 24 hours a day to process people coming into Canada, they are constantly querying databases to check traveller's information against different databases. It is extremely important for Canada to process all people entering through the border. We can protect our country and its citizens better by verifying a person is not a potential threat before letting them enter.

CBSA HQ, Ottawa, On.


Production Assurance

In the CBSA I joined the Production Assurance team in CBSA's Enterprise Services Directive to verify these technologies Border Officers use everyday work as expected. Within a border crossing there is two different categories that border officers process before allowing entry into Canada. The first category is Travellers, people coming into Canada to visit, work, study, or immigrate, the other category is cargo, goods that we import from other countries. Travellers and cargo can come into Canada in three different ways, either by land in a vehicle, by air, landing in one of Canada's Airports, or by sea, docking in a harbour. Technology is a huge factor in border operations, it enables processing to be completed quicker and helps us protect the country against potential threats. Additionally, people can make use of programs such as NEXUS and FAST, where people entering or exiting Canada on a regular bases can be pre-approved and cross through borders very quickly simply by showing a card. The role of the PA team within the agency is to test various Lines of Business that CBSA uses in daily Border operations. Within my team we focus on travellers, people entering and exiting Canada. The main goal of a software tester is to test a given product or release and to track the results of prepared test cases. When a test case did not return the expected result, we had to report defects through a web application called JIRA. We would track the progress of the defect, providing any help we could to the developers, and then we would close the ticket once the defect has been fixed

My Role

One of the most exciting projects I got to work on during my Co-op was the Primary Inspection Kiosk. PIK plays a very important role in major Airports, such as Toronto Pearson International Airport. Thousand of people use the PIK Kiosks everyday, it is important the software is updated and maintained regularly. This Kiosk is designed to be the first step when a traveller steps off the plane at an airport and allows the traveller to scan their passport and declare what they have brought with them. Have a look at the video below to gain more information about PIK, perhaps you have recently used this Kiosk during your travels.



At a high level, we had to create data for travellers and then simulate passages through Airport Kiosks to make sure foreign travellers are correctly referred to Secondary processing or if they can be released, depending on permits. To do this I had to learn how to create the data using a specific type of file structure and then push the data to the database. I got to learn how to make use of some of IBM's COTS products to assist in making data. After the data was inserted, I had to verify the data using SQL commands on various tables. We had to make sure the individual sets of data contained all permutations for the different types of permits a traveller could have. At first, I had to create the data manually, which was very tedious and time consuming. Suddenly I had a very good idea, I could create an application that automated inserting desired data into the specific file format. After consulting with my team lead and purposing the idea, I created a Java application that read data from an Excel spreadsheet, then it wrote that data into a XML file that could be injected into the testing database. This tool has the potential to be very useful, it could create a data file for us in seconds that would otherwise take longer to make manually. Furthermore, the Excel sheet made it easier to create all permutations the tester needed, and it reduced room for error that was there when typing data by hand. My team lead and other team leads were very impressed with my work and a lot of people started to make use of my Java tool. I then gave a boardroom presentation to the team members of Production Assurance along with individual demos to other testers and developers. Giving a presentation to my colleagues and sharing my work with them was very important to me and seemed like the best possible way to end my work term.

I also worked on testing the NEXUS enrolment system along with the NEXUS Kiosk used along side PIK. NEXUS is a program for Canadians and Americans in which they can be pre-screened and interviewed to make navigating through Airport border security quicker. I tested the enrolment component which is a web-application for capturing the clientâ€™s information. If the client is approved for the enrolment after an interview, they are granted a NEXUS card. The card can be scanned at a Kiosk and then the client would scan his/her irises. Testing the Iris scanner was very exciting because it is a very new technology the border is implementing. When cannabis legalization came into effect, I had to run tests to verify the Kiosk flagged people who had Cannabis Hits associated with them to make sure they would be directed to visit an Officer for further questions.



My first Co-op experience taught me a lot, I was able to design and create something from scratch that could increase productivity of data creation, a major step during testing efforts within the team. This would be the first time in my career as a software developer where I thought outside the box to create something that was going to make other people's jobs easier. I believe this is what Co-op is meant to do, it puts you in a unique position where you can experience many firsts. I was able to work on very cool projects that almost every person who enters or exits Canada will come across in some shape or form. Furthermore, I think this was a good experience for the Government of Canada because it gave a younger, fresher point of view on concepts and ideas that could lead to better products in the future.

My Goals

This being my very first Co-op, as well as my first job in an IT office, I knew there was going to be a steep learning curve. In my first two years in Computer Science I have been developing my own code and doing my own testing. In school they do not teach you how to test software, so going into a Production Assurance job would be difficult. I wanted to be an agile learner in order to be an asset to my team as fast as I could. I set many goals for myself and I feel over the 8 months I worked at the CBSA I accomplished them.

Probably the biggest part of being a software tester is analysing the documents provided to you for a given release and then come up with various test cases to validate the application. One of my main goals was learning how to write meaningful, useful, test cases. I learned the format for writing test cases that was commonly used in the government. I was able to see test cases written by other people on my team and learn how they should be properly done. I got hands on experiences with programs like SOAP UI which we used for functional testing of web services. I got to create my own scripts for testing that covered multiple permutations.

This was the first position where I had to make critical decisions on my own on what had to be done. My team lead put trust in me and did not micro manage me at all. I was very fortunate to have that luxury and I did not want to let her down. I had to create my own Test Plans for a given release and submit it to release management. I had to think for myself and estimate how many days it would take to test a release. The decisions that I made, either on my own or within a small team, impacted the overall schedule of the production date. This gave me a great sense of responsibility and I felt like what I did mattered. I found a great deal of confidence in myself toward the end of my 8-month term and the things I did were really making an impact.

Another skill I got a chance to improve was how to work well within a team in an IT environment. When testing a certain release, it was common practice to have two or more people testing. This proved very useful when writing and executing test cases because it allowed us to brainstorm the best approach to a specific project and gave the opportunity to cover more. I believe this is the biggest skill someone can learn, and it will always be necessary to work in a team at some point in any given job.
